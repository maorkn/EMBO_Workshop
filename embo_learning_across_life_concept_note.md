# EMBO Workshop Concept Note  
## Learning Across Life: Functional Components and Diverse Biological Implementations

**Working subtitle:**  
*Memory, prediction, mismatch, and adaptive action from single cells to multicellular organisms*

**Status:** Internal working draft for discussion.  
**Purpose:** Clarify the scientific scope, possible session structure, and candidate speaker list for an EMBO Workshop grant application.

---

## 1. Core idea

Learning is usually discussed through its outcomes: habituation, priming, conditioning, prediction, behavioral flexibility, cellular adaptation, immune memory, or nervous-system plasticity. The workshop should avoid becoming only a catalogue of cases where different organisms appear to learn.

Instead, the central question should be:

> **What functional components are required for a biological system to learn, and how are those components implemented differently across the tree of life?**

The premise is that any learning-like process requires some set of abstract functional components: memory, forgetting, recall, sensory input, action/output, internal modelling or state estimation, error or mismatch detection, and biases/priors in sensing and interpretation. These components may be implemented by very different biological machinery in different systems: biochemical networks, chromatin states, protein dynamics, transcriptional feedback, motility circuits, immune repertoires, plant signalling, bioelectric fields, neural circuits, or collective behaviours.

The workshop would therefore separate three levels:

1. **Function** — what must the system do for learning to occur?
2. **Mechanism** — how is that function implemented physically?
3. **Ecology/evolution** — why is that implementation useful for this organism in its environment and history?

A useful framing sentence:

> **The workshop will ask not only whether different organisms learn, but what biological architectures make learning possible.**

---

## 2. Conceptual motivation

Across biology, organisms and collectives must deal with temporally structured environments. They sense only a partial and biased version of the world, act through constrained output channels, and often need to update future responses based on previous experience. This is true for bacteria navigating chemical gradients, unicellular eukaryotes responding to repeated stress, plants integrating environmental histories, immune systems remembering pathogens, animals learning through nervous systems, and collectives coordinating distributed behaviour.

The same abstract problem appears repeatedly:

- The world has hidden structure.
- The organism receives incomplete signals.
- Past experience can improve future responses.
- Some internal state must retain, transform, or discard information.
- Actions alter both the organism and the environment.
- Mismatch between expected and actual conditions must drive updating.
- Evolutionary history shapes what the organism can sense, value, and learn.

The workshop would bring together researchers who normally sit in separate communities: systems biology, microbial physiology, developmental biology, plant behaviour, neuroscience, cognitive science, theoretical biology, philosophy of biology, and information theory.

The goal is to build a comparative framework for biological learning across scales.

## 4. Functional components to organize the workshop

### 4.1 Memory

Learning requires that past states influence future responses. Memory can be molecular, cellular, physiological, developmental, neural, ecological, or collective.

Possible implementations:

- chromatin and transcriptional memory
- protein abundance or modification states
- signalling network hysteresis
- metabolic state
- asymmetric inheritance
- immune clonal expansion
- neural synaptic change
- externalized memory in trails, structures, or modified environments

Core question:

> What counts as memory when there is no nervous system?

---

### 4.2 Forgetting and resetting

Learning systems must not remember everything forever. Forgetting can be adaptive because old information becomes misleading when environments change.

Possible implementations:

- molecular degradation
- dilution by growth or division
- active reset of signalling pathways
- epigenetic erasure
- loss of primed state
- extinction learning
- developmental transitions

Core question:

> When is forgetting a failure, and when is it a necessary component of adaptive learning?

---

### 4.3 Recall and reactivation

Stored information must be reactivated in a context-dependent way. A memory that cannot be recalled or acted upon is not useful for learning.

Possible implementations:

- cue-triggered transcriptional reactivation
- re-entry into stress-protective states
- immune secondary responses
- reactivation of latent behavioural programs
- neural recall
- attractor-state return

Core question:

> How does a biological system know which past state is relevant now?

---

### 4.4 Input channels: sensing the world

No organism learns from the world directly. It learns from the world as filtered through its sensory architecture.

Possible implementations:

- receptors and signalling pathways
- chemotaxis systems
- mechanosensation
- light sensing
- interoception
- immune recognition
- plant sensory integration
- nervous-system sensory channels

Core question:

> How do organisms transform environmental structure into usable internal signals?

---

### 4.5 Output channels: acting on the world

Learning is not only internal updating. It must change what the system does.

Possible implementations:

- gene-expression changes
- metabolic switching
- secretion
- motility
- aggregation
- developmental transitions
- immune response
- behaviour
- niche construction

Core question:

> What kinds of action are sufficient for learning-like adaptation?

---

### 4.6 Internal models, state estimation, and prediction

A biological system may contain internal structure that tracks regularities in the world. This need not be symbolic, conscious, or neural. It may be implemented as dynamical structure that makes future states partially predictable and actionable.

Possible implementations:

- gene-regulatory networks
- biochemical feedback loops
- sensory adaptation networks
- dynamical attractors
- immune repertoires
- developmental patterning systems
- neural generative models
- collective distributed computation

Core question:

> What is the weakest biologically meaningful sense of a “world model”?

---

### 4.7 Error, mismatch, loss, and biological value

Learning requires some signal that links the current internal state to success or failure in the world. In machine learning this might be called loss. In biology it may appear as stress, prediction error, reward, metabolic imbalance, homeostatic deviation, or reduced fitness proxy.

Possible implementations:

- stress-response activation
- metabolic imbalance
- homeostatic deviation
- prediction error
- reinforcement or reward
- growth-rate change
- damage accumulation
- immune failure/success
- reproductive success proxies

Core question:

> What plays the role of an error signal in living systems?

---

### 4.8 Biases, priors, and interpretation

Learning is never neutral. Organisms do not sample the world objectively. Evolutionary history, morphology, ecology, and development determine what signals are sensed, ignored, amplified, or interpreted as meaningful.

Possible implementations:

- receptor repertoires
- sensory thresholds
- evolved priors
- ecological tuning
- developmental constraints
- body-plan constraints
- lineage history
- attention-like filtering
- metabolic preferences

Core question:

> How does evolutionary history bias what an organism can learn?

## 8. Proposed workshop outcomes

### 8.1 Scientific outcome

A shared framework for comparing learning-like processes across biological systems by mapping functional components to mechanistic implementations.

### 8.2 Community outcome

A new interdisciplinary community around biological learning that includes but is not limited to neuroscience.

### 8.3 Publication outcome

Possible review/perspective:

**“Functional components of learning across life”**

or

**“A comparative biology of learning: memory, mismatch, and adaptive action across scales”**

### 8.4 Experimental outcome

Define minimal criteria for testing learning-like processes in non-neural systems:

- repeated exposure or experience
- change in future response
- persistence beyond immediate stimulation
- specificity of response
- reversibility or forgetting
- adaptive consequence
- mechanism of storage and recall
- separation from purely passive damage or selection

## 9. One-paragraph grant pitch

Learning is usually studied within specific biological domains, such as neural circuits, animal behaviour, immune memory, microbial adaptation, plant signalling, or cellular stress responses. Yet across these systems, learning-like processes may depend on a shared set of functional components: memory and forgetting, recall, sensory input, action/output channels, internal models of environmental regularities, error or mismatch signals, and evolved biases in sensing and interpretation. This workshop will ask whether these components form a general architecture for biological learning, and how this architecture is implemented differently across the tree of life. By bringing together experimentalists and theorists working on microbes, unicellular eukaryotes, plants, developmental systems, immune-like memory, nervous systems, and collective behaviour, the meeting will develop a comparative vocabulary for biological learning across scales. The goal is to separate the abstract functions required for learning from the specific molecular, cellular, neural, or ecological machinery that realizes them.

---

## 10. Shorter, sharper pitch

Rather than asking whether different organisms “really learn,” this workshop asks what functional components make learning possible. We will compare memory, forgetting, recall, sensing, action, internal models, mismatch signals, and evolved biases across biological systems, from single cells to collectives and animals. The central aim is to distinguish the general architecture of learning from the diverse biological machinery that implements it.
