## Abstract

Learning is often studied through specialized outcomes: neural plasticity, habituation, immune memory, microbial adaptation, plant signalling, or cellular stress priming. This workshop asks whether these phenomena can be compared through a shared set of functional components: memory and forgetting, recall, sensing, action, internal state estimation, prediction, mismatch signals, and evolved biases in interpretation. Rather than asking whether non-neural systems "really learn", the meeting will examine what biological architectures make learning possible and how these functions are implemented by different mechanisms across life. Sessions will bring together experimental and theoretical researchers working on microbes, unicellular eukaryotes, plants, developmental and bioelectric systems, immune-like memory, nervous systems, and collective behavior. By separating function, mechanism, and ecology/evolution, the workshop will develop a comparative vocabulary for learning-like processes across scales. The intended outcome is a framework for identifying minimal criteria for biological learning, distinguishing adaptive updating from passive damage or selection, and designing cross-system experiments that test how organisms use past experience to improve future responses.

## Reasons For Holding This Meeting

Recent work has expanded the study of learning beyond classical animal and neural systems. Microbial persistence, cellular stress memory, slime mould habituation, plant signalling, developmental bioelectricity, immune memory, collective behavior, and quantitative models of prediction and control all point to systems that use past experience to shape future responses. These fields rarely meet, and when they do, they often lack a shared vocabulary for separating learning, plasticity, adaptation, selection, and damage. This workshop will address that gap by asking which functional components are required for biological learning and how these components are implemented by different molecular, cellular, physiological, neural, and ecological mechanisms. The meeting is timely because new experimental tools now make it possible to track memory, state transitions, signalling history, and adaptive responses at single-cell and multicellular scales, while theoretical work offers formal language for prediction, mismatch, information processing, and control. The workshop will help define a comparative biology of learning across life.

## Programme Introductory Paragraph

The programme is organized to move from conceptual definition to biological implementation and then to synthesis. The opening session defines the problem of learning beyond the nervous system and establishes distinctions between learning, plasticity, adaptation, damage, and selection. The second part compares the physical substrates of memory, forgetting, sensing, and action across microbes, cells, plants, developmental systems, nervous systems, and collectives. The third part asks how biological systems estimate state, predict environmental regularities, detect mismatch, and assign biological value. The final sessions connect these mechanisms to ecological statistics and evolutionary history, ending with a synthesis discussion aimed at producing a comparative framework and experimental criteria for studying learning-like processes across life.

## Networking Activities

The meeting will include dedicated networking formats that connect early-career researchers with invited speakers and with each other. Poster sessions will be scheduled as standalone 90-minute sessions with no parallel talks, so that all participants can attend. Flash talks will give poster presenters brief visibility before poster viewing. Meet-the-speaker roundtables will be organized around scientific questions rather than career status, allowing PhD students and postdoctoral researchers to speak with invited speakers in small groups. Breakout groups will be organized by functional component - memory, sensing, action, prediction, and mismatch - rather than by organism, so that participants working on different systems are deliberately mixed. Joint lunches and dinners will be organized for all participants; no separate speakers-only dinner will be held. The final synthesis session will collect discussion outputs into a shared framework that can support future collaborations and a possible perspective or review article.

If the meeting is hybrid, virtual participants should have access to livestreamed talks, online Q&A, a virtual poster area, and at least one dedicated online meet-the-speaker or breakout session. The hybrid plan should be made concrete only after venue and AV capacity are known.
