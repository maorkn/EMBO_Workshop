# EMBO Workshop Application - Current State

## Working Meeting Concept

**Title under discussion:** Learning Across Life: Functional Components and Diverse Biological Implementations

**Working subtitle:** Memory, prediction, mismatch, and adaptive action from single cells to multicellular organisms

The workshop is framed around a comparative question:

> What functional components are required for a biological system to learn, and how are those components implemented differently across the tree of life?

The central strength of the proposal is that it does not treat the meeting as a catalogue of organisms that appear to learn. Instead, it separates three levels:

1. **Function:** what must a system do for learning to occur?
2. **Mechanism:** how is that function physically implemented?
3. **Ecology and evolution:** why is that implementation useful in the organism's environment and evolutionary history?

## Core Scientific Logic

The concept note argues that learning-like processes across life may depend on a shared set of functional components:

- memory
- forgetting and resetting
- recall and reactivation
- sensing/input channels
- action/output channels
- internal models, state estimation, and prediction
- error, mismatch, loss, or biological value signals
- evolved biases, priors, and interpretation

These functions may be implemented through very different biological machinery, including biochemical networks, chromatin states, transcriptional feedback, signalling dynamics, motility circuits, immune repertoires, plant signalling, developmental bioelectricity, neural circuits, and collective behavior.

## Proposed Scope

The workshop should cover learning and learning-like processes across:

- bacteria and microbial systems
- unicellular eukaryotes
- cellular stress responses
- developmental and bioelectric systems
- plant memory and signalling
- immune or immune-like memory
- nervous systems
- collective behavior
- theoretical and computational frameworks for biological learning

The strongest EMBO-facing version is probably a balanced but biology-heavy programme. Theory and philosophy should frame the questions, but the meeting should remain visibly rooted in life-science systems and mechanisms.

## Current Session Architecture

The concept note currently proposes eight conceptual sessions:

1. Defining learning beyond the nervous system
2. Memory, forgetting, and biological timescales
3. Input channels and biased sensing
4. Internal models and prediction without brains
5. Output channels, action, and niche construction
6. Error signals, mismatch, stress, and value
7. Ecological statistics and evolutionary priors
8. Synthesis: toward a comparative biology of learning

The most practical EMBO structure will likely compress this into a 3- or 4-day programme with invited talks, selected short talks, poster sessions, networking activities, and a final synthesis discussion.

## Current Speaker Criteria Flags

**40% underrepresented-gender criterion:** Not currently demonstrable from the current list.

The current file contains a candidate speaker map, not a final invited speaker list, and it does not include self-reported gender categories. Because EMBO evaluates this criterion on invited speakers, the application cannot yet show compliance. For planning purposes, a final list of 16 invited speakers would need at least 7 invited speakers from underrepresented genders; a final list of 18 would need at least 8. This should be checked using the gender categories provided or confirmed for the application, not by guessing from names.

**Early-career criterion:** Not currently demonstrable from the current list.

EMBO asks for a balance between early-stage researchers and established investigators, where early-stage means up to 7 years of independence. The current concept note mostly lists candidates by scientific fit and only separately flags a small set of possible ECR or younger-speaker slots. If the invited list is built mostly from the current senior/core candidate pool, it will likely be weak on this criterion. The final speaker table should include current career-stage status and should deliberately add early-stage independent group leaders, not only abstract-selected student/postdoc talks.

**Practical speaker-list implication:** Build a tracked invited-speaker table before invitations go out, with columns for session, country, EMBC/global-partner status, self-reported gender category, years since independence, confirmation status, lab URL, and relevant publication.

## EMBO Application Constraints To Keep In View

- Application deadlines are 1 March and 1 July annually.
- Applications submitted in 2026 are for meetings taking place in 2027.
- Workshops should last between 2.5 and 5 days.
- Workshops should have between 80 and 450 participants.
- At least 90% of invited speakers must be confirmed at submission.
- At least 40% of invited speakers must be from underrepresented genders.
- Some talks should be selected from abstracts, ideally at least about 50% of the number of invited talks.
- Organizers should not also be speakers.
- For a meeting in an EMBC member state, at least 50% of invited speakers should be based in EMBC member states or EMBO global partner countries.
- Poster sessions should last at least 90 minutes.
- Meetings with 150 or more participants should have at least two poster sessions.
- The application needs a draft programme using the EMBO template, saved as PDF.
- The abstract is limited to 200 words.
- The scientific rationale is limited to 200 words.
- The similar/competing meetings statement is limited to 200 words.
- Networking activities need a clear description and must appear in the programme.
- Sustainability measures should be listed.

## Strong Current Pitch

Rather than asking whether different organisms "really learn," this workshop asks what functional components make learning possible. It compares memory, forgetting, recall, sensing, action, internal models, mismatch signals, and evolved biases across biological systems, from single cells to collectives and animals. The central aim is to distinguish the general architecture of learning from the diverse biological machinery that implements it.
